Lesson 2.1: My Busy Morning Routine (Introducing Simple Present)

[cite_start]

This module introduces the second critical verb tense for A1 communication: the **Simple Present**[cite: 145]. [cite_start]At the beginner level, this tense is used specifically to discuss routines, habits, and permanent truths[cite: 146, 6].

Short Story: A Day with Sara

This story focuses on describing a third person's (Sara's) routine. [cite_start]This narrative choice forces the learner to recognize and practice the critical **'-s' ending** on the verb, which is often the most challenging conjugation rule for beginners[cite: 149, 150, 22].

Sara is a busy student. Every morning, she **wakes up** at 7:00. [cite_start]She **drinks** coffee and **eats** breakfast[cite: 153].

Sara **goes** to school at 8:00. She **studies** English. [cite_start]In the afternoon, Sara **walks** home[cite: 154].

She **watches** TV and **reads** a book. Sara **goes** to bed at 10:00. [cite_start]She **likes** her routine[cite: 155].

Grammar Explanation: The Simple Present Tense (Affirmative)

[cite_start]

The Simple Present is used to describe actions that happen regularly, such as daily routines or habits[cite: 162, 22]. [cite_start]We use this tense to talk about things that are always true or things that happen repeatedly (e.g., *She goes to school every day.*)[cite: 164].

The Verb Form Rules:

[cite_start]

The verb form depends on the subject pronoun, but the pattern is predictable[cite: 166]:

    [cite_start]
  1. **I, You, We, They:** The verb remains in its **base form** (the dictionary form)[cite: 167].

    Example: I walk home. [cite_start]We eat breakfast. [cite: 169]

    2. [cite_start]
  2. **He, She, It:** This is the most important rule[cite: 170]. [cite_start]You must **add -s or -es** to the end of the verb[cite: 171].

    Examples from the story: Sara **wakes up** (She wakes up). [cite_start]She **goes** to school. [cite: 172]

Key Spelling Tip:

[cite_start]

Most verbs just add **'-s'** (drink $\rightarrow$ drinks; study $\rightarrow$ studies)[cite: 173]. [cite_start]However, verbs that end in sounds like 'sh', 'ch', 'o', or 'x' often add **'-es'** (watch $\rightarrow$ watches; go $\rightarrow$ goes)[cite: 174]. [cite_start]This distinction is necessary for accurate A1 usage[cite: 175, 22].

**Note on Simplification:** This introductory lesson focuses only on the affirmative (positive) forms of the Simple Present. [cite_start]The negative and question forms are typically introduced in a subsequent lesson to manage the cognitive load for beginners[cite: 176, 22].
